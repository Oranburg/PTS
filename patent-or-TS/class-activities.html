Activity 1

Building Your Decision Framework

15 minutes Individual

Students independently work through a structured 5-step framework for analyzing whether an innovation should be protected via patent or kept as a trade secret. This exercise builds analytical habits before engaging in group discussion.

The 5-Step Analysis Framework

1

Identify the Innovation

What exactly is being protected? Is it a formula, a process, an algorithm, a design, or a combination? Precision here determines which legal framework applies.

Ask: Can I describe it in a single sentence with specificity?

2

Assess Patentability

Is the innovation novel, non-obvious, and useful? Would it survive a prior art search? Is it in a patent-eligible category (not an abstract idea or natural phenomenon)?

Ask: Could we actually get a patent granted, and what would it cost?

3

Evaluate Reverse Engineering Risk

Once the product is on the market, could a skilled competitor figure out the innovation by analyzing it? Trade secret protection evaporates if the secret becomes known through legitimate means.

Ask: Is our secret actually keep-able once we ship?

4

Consider Duration Needs

A utility patent lasts 20 years from filing — excellent for technologies with a defined product lifecycle. Trade secrets can last indefinitely but require ongoing maintenance of secrecy measures.

Ask: Is 20 years enough — or do we need protection forever?

5

Weigh Disclosure vs. Secrecy

Filing a patent requires full public disclosure. Does that disclosure give competitors a roadmap? Or does a patent's deterrence value outweigh the cost of disclosure?

Ask: Does publishing this hurt us more than the monopoly right helps us?

Describe a real or hypothetical innovation and work through each step.

Activity 2

Patent vs. Trade Secret Debate

30 minutes Teams of 4–6

The class is divided into two teams: Team Patent and Team Trade Secret. Each team argues their assigned position for a chosen scenario — regardless of personal opinion. This adversarial format develops stronger analytical skills.

Team Patent

Argue that the innovation should be protected through the patent system.

  • Strong exclusivity right
  • Defensive patent portfolio value
  • Licensing revenue opportunity
  • Protection if secret leaks

Team Trade Secret

Argue that the innovation should be protected as a confidential trade secret.

  • Potentially unlimited duration
  • No public disclosure required
  • Immediate protection, no filing
  • No prosecution costs

Debate Structure & Timing

5 min Teams prepare arguments and assign speakers
3 min Team Patent presents their case — uninterrupted
3 min Team Trade Secret presents their case — uninterrupted
2 min Rebuttals — each team responds to the other's strongest point
7 min Open class discussion and instructor-led analysis

Debate Scenarios

A

Pharmaceutical Synthesis Process

A company discovers a new synthesis process for a blockbuster drug

Intermediate

Scenario Facts

PharmaCo has discovered a novel multi-step synthesis process for producing DrugX — a blockbuster medication projected to generate $2B/year. The process reduces production cost by 40%. The drug itself will be FDA-approved and the active compound is separately patentable. Should the synthesis process be patented or kept as a trade secret?

Key Patent Arguments

  • Prevents generic manufacturers from copying the process post-expiry
  • FDA manufacturing disclosures may inadvertently reveal the process anyway
  • Licensing to other pharma = revenue stream

Key Trade Secret Arguments

  • Chemical processes are extremely hard to reverse-engineer exactly
  • Trade secret can outlast the 20-year patent period
  • Patent disclosure is a competitor roadmap once the patent expires
B

AI Recommendation Engine

A tech startup builds a personalized recommendation model

Advanced

Scenario Facts

StartupAI has built a recommendation engine that processes anonymized user behavioral data to predict purchasing intent with 94% accuracy — significantly outperforming competitors. The model runs server-side and is never distributed. It took 18 months and $4M to develop. Should they patent the method or keep it as a trade secret?

Key Patent Arguments

  • Creates investor/acquirer-friendly IP portfolio
  • Provides defense if a competitor patents similar method first
  • AI/ML patents are increasingly valuable for licensing

Key Trade Secret Arguments

  • Server-side model is impossible to reverse-engineer from API
  • Training data + model weights = the real secret, not the method
  • Patent abstract-idea rejections (Alice doctrine) make AI patents risky
C

Unique Sauce Flavor Profile

A food company creates a proprietary flavor combination

Beginner

Scenario Facts

FoodCo has developed a proprietary spice blend and fermentation process that creates a unique flavor profile for their signature hot sauce. Food scientists could potentially reverse-engineer the flavor via gas chromatography analysis. The product will be sold at retail and the recipe is held by one employee. Patent or trade secret?

Key Patent Arguments

  • If reverse-engineered, patent prevents imitation for 20 years
  • One employee holding the recipe = catastrophic key-person risk
  • Brand story: "Patented Formula" can be a marketing asset

Key Trade Secret Arguments

  • Coca-Cola model: 133+ years vs. a 20-year patent
  • Flavor formulas are famously hard to pin down exactly
  • No disclosure = no roadmap for competitors post-patent

Activity 3

Real-World Case Studies

20 minutes Small Groups

Analyze how famous companies actually handled the patent vs. trade secret decision. Small groups are assigned one case each, then share findings with the class.

Case Study 1

Coca-Cola Formula

Trade Secret · Since 1891

The Coca-Cola formula — "Merchandise 7X" — has been a trade secret for over 133 years. The formula is reportedly stored in a vault at the World of Coca-Cola museum in Atlanta, with access restricted to a handful of executives.

Key Insight

Sometimes patience beats patents. A 20-year patent would have expired in 1911. Instead, 133+ years of protection — and the mystique of the secret is itself a brand asset.

Discussion Questions

  1. Could a competitor reverse-engineer the formula today? Does it matter?
  2. What "reasonable measures" does Coca-Cola likely take to protect the formula?
  3. Is the formula itself, or the brand, the real competitive advantage?
Case Study 2

Google PageRank

Hybrid Strategy · Patent + Trade Secret

Google's core PageRank algorithm was patented (US6285999B1, filed 1998, expired 2018). However, the actual search ranking system used today is vastly more complex — thousands of signals kept as trade secrets — making the patent disclosure largely a historical artifact.

Key Insight

Use patents for defensive protection + trade secrets for competitive advantage. The patent deterred copyists; the trade secret kept the algorithm's actual implementation secret forever.

Discussion Questions

  1. Why patent the core algorithm if the real advantage is in the implementation?
  2. What happened when the PageRank patent expired in 2018?
  3. How does Google's approach inform a hybrid IP strategy for your client?
Case Study 3

KFC's 11 Herbs & Spices

Trade Secret · Structural Secrecy

KFC's recipe is split between two separate suppliers — neither has the complete formula. The blended spices are combined at facilities that don't know each other's portions. Colonel Sanders reportedly memorized the recipe and never wrote it down in one place.

Key Insight

Operational procedures can be structured to maintain secrecy — even when the ultimate product is available for analysis. Trade secret law protects the organizational method, not just the formula itself.

Discussion Questions

  1. Does splitting a recipe between suppliers qualify as "reasonable measures" under DTSA?
  2. What would happen legally if one supplier accidentally combined the full recipe?
  3. Could a competitor reverse-engineer the recipe and use it legally?

Key Teaching Points from the Cases

Duration Asymmetry

Patents give 20-year monopolies. Trade secrets can last indefinitely — if the secret holds. The Coca-Cola formula demonstrates this asymmetry dramatically.

Hybrid Is Valid

Patent the framework; trade-secret the implementation. Google proved that these aren't mutually exclusive. Strategic layering is often the optimal answer.

Structure Enables Secrecy

KFC shows that operational design — how you organize who knows what — is a legitimate and powerful trade secret protection mechanism.

A lab-engineered gene sequence that grants corn crops resistance to a specific class of herbicides. The sequence is novel and has passed initial efficacy trials. Competitors are working on similar approaches.

Quick Reference: Key Legal Standards

DTSA

Defend Trade Secrets Act (2016)

Federal statute enabling trade secret owners to bring civil claims in federal court. First federal civil trade secret law.

  • Civil and criminal remedies available
  • Ex parte seizure orders possible
  • Protects whistleblowers via immunity provision
  • Treble damages + attorney fees for willful misappropriation

UTSA

Uniform Trade Secrets Act (State Level)

Adopted (with variations) by 48 states + D.C. Three elements must be proven to establish trade secret status.

  • Information — must have economic value
  • Secrecy — not generally known or readily ascertainable
  • Reasonable measures — owner actively maintains secrecy
  • NY and MA have not adopted UTSA

Patent Requirements

35 U.S.C. §§ 101, 102, 103, 112

Four core requirements under U.S. patent law. Failure on any one bars patent protection entirely.

  • Novel — not in prior art (§ 102)
  • Non-obvious — to a PHOSITA (§ 103)
  • Useful — has utility (§ 101)
  • Enabled — disclosure teaches how to make/use (§ 112)

Misappropriation

vs. Proper Reverse Engineering

Not all competitive intelligence is misappropriation. Understanding the boundary is critical.

  • Misappropriation: breach of duty, theft, espionage, inducing breach
  • Permitted: independent development
  • Permitted: reverse engineering a lawfully obtained product
  • Permitted: studying publicly disclosed information

Common Misconceptions to Address

"A trade secret only protects against theft"

Correct this: trade secrets are lost if the secret becomes generally known through any means, including legitimate reverse engineering or accidental disclosure. Theft (misappropriation) is just one scenario.

"Patents always give better protection"

Patents expire; trade secrets don't. Patent prosecution is expensive and uncertain. And patents require disclosure — which competitors can use to design around your claims after expiry.

"Server-side code can't be patented"

Patentability depends on the method, not its deployment. A method of doing X can be patented even if X runs server-side. The Alice framework is the relevant obstacle, not server deployment.

"Marking something 'Confidential' creates a trade secret"

A label alone is insufficient. Courts require actual reasonable measures: NDAs, access controls, employee training, compartmentalization. The label is evidence, not the substance.

Assessment Ideas

IP Memo (Activity 4) — Grade using the rubric. Works well as an individual take-home assignment with a 1-week deadline.
Debate Participation — Assess argument quality, responsiveness to opposing points, and correct use of legal doctrine.
Case Study Write-Up — One-page analysis of a real-world case not covered in class (student-selected with instructor approval).
Multiple Choice Quiz — 10-question quiz on DTSA elements, patent requirements, and misappropriation definitions. Use as a pre- or post-test.

Discussion Facilitation Tips

Socratic Questioning Starters

  • "What would happen to this argument if the competitor could reverse-engineer it in six months?"
  • "If you were the competitor's lawyer, how would you attack that position?"
  • "Does it matter whether the secret was discovered through good work or bad luck?"

When the Debate Gets Stuck

If teams are too agreeable, introduce a curveball: "Now assume the company is planning to license the technology to three competitors. Does that change the analysis?" Licensing often breaks the impasse.

Closing the Loop

Always close with: "What more information would you need before making a final recommendation?" This teaches students that real IP decisions are always made under uncertainty.
