Case 1A: BioPharma FDA Disclosure

Level 1: Simple ⭐
~7 min

Student Facts

MedTech Innovations has developed a novel monoclonal antibody therapeutic for treating autoimmune diseases. The antibody targets a specific protein receptor, and the company has identified the precise amino acid sequence that provides therapeutic efficacy.

To bring this therapeutic to market, MedTech must submit a Biologics License Application (BLA) to the FDA, which requires full disclosure of the antibody's structure, manufacturing process, and clinical trial data.

Should MedTech protect this technology as a patent or trade secret?

Teaching Guidance

Socratic Approach: Ask students: "What happens when you submit to the FDA?" Let them identify the disclosure requirement. Then ask: "Can you maintain something as a trade secret if it's publicly disclosed?"

Expected Student Response: Students should recognize that FDA disclosure defeats trade secret protection.

Common Mistake: Some students may suggest keeping it as a trade secret and not disclosing to FDA (illegal and impossible to market).

Answer: Must Patent

Conclusion:

MedTech cannot maintain this as a trade secret and must patent if it wants any IP protection. FDA disclosure requirements make trade secret protection impossible.

Key Reasoning:
  • Regulatory Disclosure Requirement: FDA requires full disclosure of antibody structure, manufacturing, and clinical data
  • Public Disclosure Defeats Trade Secret: Once disclosed to FDA (public agency), information is no longer secret
  • No Reasonable Efforts to Maintain Secrecy: Cannot satisfy trade secret requirement when regulatory disclosure is mandatory
  • Patent Remains Available: Patent protection is designed for disclosed inventions
Doctrinal Analysis:

Trade Secret Requirements (UTSA):

  • Information must not be "generally known"
  • Must have "independent economic value from not being known"
  • Must make "reasonable efforts to maintain secrecy"
  • FAILS: FDA disclosure makes information generally known

Patent Requirements (35 U.S.C.):

  • Patent-eligible subject matter (§ 101): Therapeutic antibody qualifies
  • Novelty (§ 102): Assuming novel sequence
  • Non-obviousness (§ 103): Requires analysis of prior art
  • Enablement (§ 112): FDA submission provides this
  • LIKELY SATISFIES (assuming novelty and non-obviousness)
Teaching Point:

This case illustrates a fundamental limitation: regulatory disclosure requirements can make trade secret protection impossible. This is common in pharmaceuticals, medical devices, food additives, and other FDA-regulated products. The choice is not "patent or trade secret" but rather "patent or no IP protection."

Case 1B: Customer List Protection

Level 1: Simple ⭐
~7 min

Student Facts

PharmaSales Corp has spent 10 years developing relationships with hospital procurement officers across the United States. The company maintains a detailed database containing:

  • Names and contact information of key decision-makers
  • Purchasing patterns and preferences
  • Budget cycles and approval processes
  • Notes on personal relationships and communication preferences

This customer list gives PharmaSales a significant competitive advantage. The company keeps the database on a secure server with limited access.

Should PharmaSales protect this customer list as a patent or trade secret?

Teaching Guidance

Socratic Approach: Ask: "What is patentable subject matter?" Then: "Is a customer list a process, machine, manufacture, or composition of matter?" Then: "Could this be a trade secret?"

Expected Student Response: Students should recognize patent-eligibility limitation but may correctly identify trade secret potential.

Teaching Point: This introduces the concept that some valuable business information cannot be patented but may qualify as trade secrets.

Answer: Cannot Patent; May Be Trade Secret

Conclusion:

PharmaSales cannot patent a customer list (not patent-eligible subject matter), but may protect it as a trade secret if all requirements are met.

Key Reasoning:
  • Not Patent-Eligible: Customer lists are not processes, machines, manufactures, or compositions of matter (35 U.S.C. § 101)
  • May Be Trade Secret: Customer lists are classic examples of protectable trade secrets
  • Economic Value: Provides competitive advantage (saves time and cost of developing relationships)
  • Not Generally Known: Specific details about purchasing patterns and relationships are not public
  • Reasonable Security Measures: Secure server with limited access satisfies this requirement
Doctrinal Analysis:

Patent Eligibility (35 U.S.C. § 101):

  • Patentable: "process, machine, manufacture, or composition of matter"
  • Customer list is information/data, not a statutory category
  • FAILS patent eligibility threshold

Trade Secret Analysis (UTSA):

  • Information of any type: ✓ Customer lists qualify
  • Economic value from secrecy: ✓ Saves time/cost of relationship development
  • Not generally known: ✓ Specific purchasing patterns and notes are not public
  • Reasonable efforts to maintain secrecy: ✓ Secure server, limited access
  • LIKELY QUALIFIES as trade secret
Important Caveat:

Trade secret protection for customer lists is limited. If the information is readily ascertainable through public sources (e.g., hospital websites list procurement officers), it may not qualify. The key is whether the compilation and details (purchasing patterns, preferences, relationship notes) provide independent economic value.

Teaching Point:

This case illustrates that not all valuable business information is patent-eligible. Trade secret law fills this gap by protecting information that has economic value from secrecy. Customer lists are a classic example, but protection requires actual secrecy and reasonable security measures.

Case 1C: Abstract Software Algorithm

Level 1: Simple ⭐
~7 min

Student Facts

FinTech Solutions has developed a novel algorithm for detecting fraudulent credit card transactions. The algorithm uses statistical analysis of transaction patterns to identify anomalies.

The algorithm is implemented in software that runs on FinTech's servers (not distributed to clients). The algorithm itself is a mathematical formula that could be described in a few pages of equations.

Under Alice Corp. v. CLS Bank (2014), software patents for abstract ideas are difficult to obtain unless they involve a technological improvement.

Should FinTech protect this algorithm as a patent or trade secret?

Teaching Guidance

Socratic Approach: Ask: "What did Alice hold about abstract algorithms?" Then: "Is this algorithm deployed in a way that could be kept secret?" This bridges to Level 2 analysis.

Expected Student Response: Students should recognize Alice limitation on software patents and identify that server-side deployment enables trade secret protection.

Teaching Point: This case introduces the interaction between patent eligibility doctrine (Alice) and trade secret viability (deployment method).

Answer: Likely Not Patentable; Trade Secret Depends on Deployment

Conclusion:

FinTech likely cannot patent this algorithm under Alice, but may protect it as a trade secret because it runs on FinTech's servers (not distributed to clients).

Key Reasoning:
  • Alice Limitation: Abstract mathematical algorithms are not patent-eligible unless tied to technological improvement
  • Statistical Analysis is Abstract: Analyzing transaction patterns is a fundamental economic practice
  • No Clear Technological Improvement: Facts don't indicate improvement to computer functioning
  • Server-Side Deployment Enables Secrecy: Algorithm not disclosed to clients, so trade secret viable
  • Economic Value from Secrecy: Competitors cannot replicate without access to algorithm
Doctrinal Analysis:

Patent Eligibility After Alice (35 U.S.C. § 101):

  • Step 1: Is it an abstract idea? YES - statistical analysis of patterns
  • Step 2: Does it contain an "inventive concept" that transforms it into patent-eligible application?
  • Need: technological improvement to computer functioning OR integration with specific machine
  • Here: No indication of technological improvement
  • LIKELY FAILS under Alice

Trade Secret Analysis (UTSA):

  • Information: ✓ Algorithm qualifies
  • Economic value from secrecy: ✓ Competitors cannot replicate
  • Not generally known: ✓ Runs on FinTech's servers only
  • Reasonable efforts: ✓ Server-side deployment provides security
  • QUALIFIES as trade secret
Strategic Consideration:

Even if a patent were available, trade secret might be preferable here because:

  • Server-side deployment makes secrecy feasible
  • Patent would require disclosure of the algorithm
  • Competitors could design around a patent
  • Trade secret provides indefinite protection (vs. 20 years)
Teaching Point:

This case introduces the Alice limitation on software patents and shows how deployment method affects trade secret viability. Server-side software can be kept secret; client-side software cannot. This distinction becomes critical in Level 2 cases.

Case 2A: Chemical Sweetener

Level 2: Intermediate ⭐⭐
~10 min

Student Facts

FlavorTech Industries has synthesized a new artificial sweetener that is 500 times sweeter than sugar with no bitter aftertaste. The molecule has a unique structure that provides this property.

FlavorTech plans to sell the sweetener to food manufacturers. Once in commercial products, competitors could purchase the product and use standard analytical chemistry techniques (mass spectrometry, NMR spectroscopy) to determine the molecular structure within 3-6 months.

The compound is novel and non-obvious. FlavorTech estimates it would take competitors 18 months to independently develop a similar sweetener without reverse engineering.

Should FlavorTech protect this sweetener as a patent or trade secret?

Teaching Guidance

Socratic Approach: Ask: "How long can FlavorTech maintain this as a secret once it's sold?" Then: "What happens after 6 months?" Then: "How does that compare to patent protection?"

Key Teaching Point: This is the classic case where trade secret is not viable due to ease of reverse engineering. Must patent or lose protection entirely after 6 months.

Answer: MUST PATENT

Conclusion:

FlavorTech must patent. Trade secret protection is not viable because the molecular structure can be easily reverse-engineered within 3-6 months of commercial sale.

Key Reasoning:
  • Easy Reverse Engineering: Standard analytical chemistry can determine structure in 3-6 months
  • Commercial Distribution Enables Access: Once sold, competitors can purchase and analyze
  • Trade Secret Will Be Lost: After reverse engineering, no longer secret
  • Patent Provides 20 Years: vs. only 6 months of trade secret protection
  • Patent Requirements Satisfied: Novel, non-obvious, patentable subject matter
Economic Analysis:

Comparison of Protection Duration:

  • Trade Secret: 6 months (until reverse engineered) → Then no protection
  • Patent: 20 years of exclusivity
  • Cost of Reverse Engineering: Low (standard analytical techniques)
  • Value of 20-Year Exclusivity: Enormous in food ingredient market
Real-World Context:

This scenario mirrors actual cases like aspartame (NutraSweet) and sucralose (Splenda), which were patented because their molecular structures could be easily determined through analysis. Chemical compounds sold commercially are almost always patented for this reason.

Teaching Point:

This case illustrates the fundamental principle: if reverse engineering is easy, trade secret protection is not viable. Chemical compounds sold commercially are classic examples where patents are essential because analytical techniques can reveal structure quickly.

Case 4A: Biotech Therapeutic Platform

Level 4: Expert ⭐⭐⭐⭐
~15 min

Student Facts

BioTherapeutics Inc. has developed a comprehensive platform for discovering and manufacturing therapeutic antibodies. The platform includes 7 key components:

  1. Target Discovery Algorithm: AI system for identifying disease targets
  2. Antibody Design Method: Computational approach for designing antibodies
  3. Proprietary Cell Line: Engineered cells for antibody production
  4. Bioreactor Control System: Automated system for optimizing production
  5. Purification Process: Novel method for purifying antibodies
  6. Quality Control Assays: Testing methods for ensuring product quality
  7. Platform Brand: "BioTherapeutics Discovery Platform"

BioTherapeutics plans to use this platform internally and also license it to other biotech companies.

How should BioTherapeutics protect each component of this technology stack?

Teaching Guidance

Socratic Approach: Go through each component and ask: "What IP tools are available for this?" Encourage students to consider patents, trade secrets, copyright, trademark, and contracts.

Expected Discussion: Students should recognize that different components require different strategies. Some need patents, some trade secrets, some both, some additional tools.

Key Teaching Point: Real-world IP strategy requires using multiple tools together. No single approach works for complex technology stacks.

Answer: Component-by-Component Mixed Strategy

1. Target Discovery Algorithm (AI System)

Strategy: Trade Secret + Patent (Defensive) + Copyright

  • Primary: Trade secret (algorithm details, training data, weights)
  • Secondary: Patent specific novel methods (defensive, prevent competitors from patenting)
  • Tertiary: Copyright for software code
  • Reasoning: AI algorithms difficult to reverse engineer if deployed server-side; patents provide defensive protection; copyright protects implementation
2. Antibody Design Method

Strategy: Patent + Trade Secret (Optimization Details)

  • Primary: Patent the core design method
  • Secondary: Trade secret for optimization techniques and parameters
  • Reasoning: Method patents valuable for licensing; optimization details can remain secret
3. Proprietary Cell Line

Strategy: Trade Secret + Patent (if genetic modifications) + Material Transfer Agreement (MTA)

  • Primary: Trade secret (physical control of cell line)
  • Secondary: Patent any novel genetic modifications
  • Tertiary: MTA with strict terms for any transfers
  • Reasoning: Physical cell lines can be kept secret; genetic modifications may be patentable; MTAs provide contractual protection
4. Bioreactor Control System

Strategy: Trade Secret + Patent (Hardware) + Copyright (Software)

  • Primary: Trade secret for control algorithms and parameters
  • Secondary: Patent novel hardware components
  • Tertiary: Copyright for software
  • Reasoning: Control parameters difficult to reverse engineer; hardware may be patentable; software copyrightable
5. Purification Process

Strategy: Hybrid (Patent Core Method + Trade Secret Details)

  • Primary: Patent the novel purification method
  • Secondary: Trade secret for specific parameters and optimization
  • Reasoning: Method patent provides broad protection; specific conditions can remain secret
6. Quality Control Assays

Strategy: Patent + Trade Secret (Protocols)

  • Primary: Patent novel assay methods
  • Secondary: Trade secret for detailed protocols and acceptance criteria
  • Reasoning: Assay methods patentable; specific protocols can remain proprietary
7. Platform Brand

Strategy: Trademark + Trade Dress

  • Primary: Trademark for "BioTherapeutics Discovery Platform"
  • Secondary: Trade dress for distinctive visual elements
  • Reasoning: Brand protection essential for market differentiation; indefinite protection if maintained
Licensing Considerations:

Since BioTherapeutics plans to license this platform, the IP strategy must balance:

  • Disclosure Requirements: Licensees need enough information to use the platform
  • Protection Needs: Must prevent licensees from becoming competitors
  • Enforcement: Patents easier to enforce than trade secrets in licensing context
  • Layered Protection: Use patents for core innovations, trade secrets for optimization, contracts for additional restrictions
Teaching Point:

This case demonstrates that complex technology platforms require integrated IP strategies using multiple tools. Different components have different optimal protection strategies. Real-world IP counsel must analyze each component separately and design a comprehensive protection plan that considers business model (internal use vs. licensing).

Case 4B: AI Analytics Platform (SaaS)

Level 4: Expert ⭐⭐⭐⭐

AI Analytics Corp has developed a SaaS platform with 7 components including core engine, ML models, training data, UI, API, client-side library, and brand...

Answer: SaaS-Specific Strategy with Selective Open Source

Server-side components: Trade secret + defensive patents. Client-side: Consider open source for community building. Brand: Trademark. SaaS model enables strong trade secret protection.

Key Takeaways

Decision Factors

  • Reverse engineering feasibility
  • Independent discovery likelihood
  • Regulatory disclosure requirements
  • Enforcement costs and feasibility
  • Duration needs (20 years vs. indefinite)

Strategic Principles

  • Context matters: technology, industry, market
  • Multiple IP tools can be used together
  • No single right answer for complex cases
  • Quality of reasoning matters most
  • Component-by-component analysis for tech stacks
